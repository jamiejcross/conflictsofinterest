Conflict of Interest Policies in Academic Publishing

A textual analysis of conflict-of-interest policies across 50 global-health, health-policy and social-medicine journals.
50 journals · 12 distinct policy frameworks · 7 declaration dimensions

Conflicts of interest do not refer to the same thing across all academic journals. This website provides a systematic comparison of policies across a corpus of academic publications relevant to global health, health-policy and social-medicine.

The fifty journals in this corpus do not operate fifty conflict-of-interest regimes. They operate roughly a dozen. Almost all sit on top of the ICMJE Disclosure Form and the COPE guidance, with publisher houses (Elsevier, BMJ, Wiley, SAGE, Taylor & Francis, BMC/Springer Nature, Oxford, MDPI, Frontiers) layering their own examples on top. Reading the policy texts side by side, the striking pattern is of a shared shape. They present an exhaustive list of financial relationships, a precise demand for funding statements, and then a single elastic catch-all — “any other personal or professional relationship that could be perceived to influence the work” — into which everything awkward is folded.

This website scores each Conflict of Interest framework against seven declaration dimensions — study funding, in-kind contributions, individual shares in companies, prior work experience with companies, personal work contacts, gifts received from companies, and invitations from professional associations — distinguishing what policies name explicitly, what they leave partial (subsumed under a broad category, or bounded by an inconsistent time window), and where they are effectively silent and rely on author discretion.

How to read this: the two items most readily associated with “conflict of interest” in the abstract — money for the study, and shares in a company — are exactly the two that are already tightly specified. 3 · Where the policies are already tight

Study funding — universal and specific

Every framework in the corpus requires a funding statement, and the better ones go further than naming the funder: the ICMJE recommendation insists on separating direct support for the work from general institutional support for an author's time, and on describing the funder's role in design, data, writing and the decision to publish. MDPI, Frontiers, SAGE and Elsevier all carry an explicit “the funder had no role…” template. There is little to tighten here except enforcement.

Individual shares — explicit almost everywhere

“Stock ownership or options” (ICMJE), “holding stocks or shares” (BMC), “stocks, shares, patents…” (SAGE), “stock or share ownership” (T&F) — equity is the single most consistently named non-funding item, explicit in 42 of 50 journals. The one substantive loophole worth flagging is Elsevier's de minimis carve-out, which excludes holdings below 1% of a publicly traded company or held through mutual/pension funds. That is defensible for large-cap exposure but creates a bright-line exemption that a determined author can sit just underneath.

4 · Where the policies thin out — and how to tighten them

Gifts received from companies — systematic silence

Not one framework in the corpus names gifts or hospitality as a disclosable category. The word does not appear in the ICMJE recommendation, the BMJ examples, the SAGE or T&F lists, or the MDPI and Elsevier enumerations. Gifts are presumably meant to fall under “any other relationship that could be perceived to influence the work” — but a meal, a conference dinner, sponsored travel, or a piece of branded equipment does not read to most authors as a “relationship,” and so goes undeclared. This is the clearest single opportunity in the corpus.

  • Name gifts and hospitality as their own line item, distinct from honoraria and consulting fees, with a worked example (“e.g. sponsored travel to a meeting, meals, accommodation, branded equipment, or other benefits in kind from a commercial entity”).
  • Set a de minimis threshold rather than relying on author judgement of “significance” — borrow the convention from clinical guidelines (e.g. cumulative value over a stated sum within 36 months).

In-kind contributions — named by a minority

Only BMJ (“receipt of equipment or supplies”), Wiley (“drugs/equipment supplied…, travel paid…, writing assistance, administrative support”) and Taylor & Francis (“receipt of drugs, specialist equipment, tools, computer programs…writing assistance or administrative support”) name in-kind support explicitly — 12 journals in all. A further 23 capture it only obliquely through “reimbursements” or “other funding”; Elsevier, MDPI, Frontiers, Oxford and Policy Press are effectively silent. For ethnographic and policy research in particular, in-kind support (access to a field site, seconded staff, data, software, facilitation) is often the only material relationship with an interested party, and it falls straight through a funding-centric form.

  • Replace “funding” with “funding and material support” in the statement prompt, and enumerate non-cash forms: equipment, software, data access, facilities, seconded personnel, writing/editorial assistance, and travel.
  • Require the role-of-supporter statement to extend to in-kind providers, not just cash funders — the body that lent the equipment may have the same interest as the body that wrote the cheque.

Invitations from professional associations — mostly partial or silent

Paid lectures, speakers' bureaux and honoraria are captured by most frameworks, but unpaid invitations — keynote slots, committee seats, advisory or honorary roles offered by professional associations and learned societies — are named explicitly only by SAGE, which lists “members of a research advisory board, steering or advisory committees, associations or honorary affiliations” and “membership of organisations or scientific societies that undertake advocacy work.” Fifteen journals are wholly silent. The reputational pull of a prestigious unpaid invitation is real and is exactly the kind of secondary interest the policies claim to be about.

  • Adopt SAGE's wording as a sector template: name advisory boards, steering committees, honorary roles and society memberships — and make clear these are disclosable whether or not they are remunerated.
  • Distinguish advocacy-oriented bodies (whose positions may bear directly on the research) from neutral learned societies, so that the declaration is informative rather than a box-tick.

Previous work experience with companies — universally partial, inconsistently bounded

Employment is named everywhere, but almost always in the present tense, and the look-back windows are wildly inconsistent: ICMJE asks for relationships in the general area over the 36 months before submission; SAGE uses a five-year window; Oxford asks for consultancies within 12 months. The result is that the same prior post at a company would be declarable at one journal and not another, and authors moving between journals cannot form a stable habit of disclosure.

  • State a single explicit look-back period for prior employment and consultancy (the ICMJE 36-month standard is the obvious anchor) and apply it consistently.
  • Name “former employment or consultancy” as a distinct prompt rather than leaving it to authors to infer that a past role counts as a current “relationship.”

Personal work contacts — elastic clauses

Every framework gestures at “personal or professional relationships,” “rivalries,” or “close working relationships,” but the wording is uniformly soft and the threshold is left entirely to the author. SAGE again goes furthest (“close relatives,” “close working relationships with editors, editorial board members”) and Frontiers operationalises it best — but only for editors and reviewers, in a structured questionnaire (spouse, close friend, recent co-author, supervisor/student within three years). Authors face no equivalent structured prompt.

  • Port Frontiers' reviewer questionnaire to the author side: convert the catch-all into specific yes/no prompts (recent co-authorship, shared institution, supervisory relationship, family tie) with stated time bounds.
  • Define the threshold for a declarable personal contact, rather than asking authors to self-assess what “could be perceived” as influential.

5 · A note on the limits of disclosure

Tightening wording closes the gaps catalogued above, but it does not resolve the deeper design choice these policies share. They are transparency instruments, not management instruments: with the partial exception of editorial recusal, almost every framework states that a declared interest will not, in itself, prevent publication. BMJ is explicit — “we will not reject papers simply because authors have a competing interest.” Disclosure thus shifts the interpretive burden onto the reader, who is presumed able to weigh an interest they can see. The seven dimensions examined here are, in effect, the items the current architecture renders least visible — and so the items on which the reader is least equipped to judge. The strongest version of “tightening” is therefore not only longer lists but structured, item-by-item prompts (the Frontiers model) that remove discretion from the point of disclosure, where it currently sits.

Method & caveats. Coverage levels reflect the wording of each publisher's generic conflict-of-interest / competing-interest policy as published on its author-guidance pages (verified June 2026), plus the ICMJE Recommendations and Disclosure Form on which the majority depend. Individual journals occasionally add stricter requirements at the title level that are not captured here; where a journal's stated policy was simply to follow ICMJE/COPE, it is scored at the ICMJE/COPE baseline. “Explicit / Partial / Silent” refer to whether the named item appears in the policy text, not to how rigorously any journal enforces it. This is a reading of policy language, not an audit of published disclosures.
